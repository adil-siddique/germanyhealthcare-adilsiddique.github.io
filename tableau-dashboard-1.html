Germany Healthcare Overview Dashboard

Key Insights

  • Germany has a high population in Europe (85 million); 22% of the population is aged 65+.
  • Public and Non-Profit hospital together account for 72% of the total hospital, 80.5% of total beds and about 79% of all surgery.
  • Germany has excellent healthcare infrastructure: 2,300 hospitals with a total of 507K beds (599 beds / 100K). According to WHO guidance (300–500 beds / 100K), Germany is well above the upper limit.
  • Germany also has the highest surgical volume in Europe with 19 million surgeries in 2023 (22K per 100K population).
  • An aging population will drive growth in orthopedics, cardiology, oncology, chronic care, and rehabilitation; high surgical volume also increases demand for medical consumables.
  • With excellent healthcare infrastructure, universal coverage, and an aging population, Germany experiences consistently high surgical volumes.
  • Nordrhein-Westfalen, Bayern and Baden-Württemberg are the three key states: highly urbanized with a large number of specialty and general-care centres. These three states contribute 50.5% (9.6 million) of total surgeries in 2023.
  • Nordrhein-Westfalen includes major urban areas such as Bochum, Cologne, Essen, Düsseldorf, Dortmund, whose employment-driven population inflows increase baseline demand for acute and elective care.
  • In Bayern, Munich alone performs 22% of the state’s surgeries.
  • Baden-Württemberg contains key urban centres such as Stuttgart, Freiburg, Karlsruhe, and Heidelberg.
  • Berlin (the capital) and the two city-states Hamburg and Bremen are also strategically important.
  • Berlin is densely populated, has a highly developed healthcare system and a very high per capita surgery rate; it draws patients from surrounding Brandenburg and internationally for specialized care. High clinical-trial activity and specialist services drive procedure volumes and early adoption of new technologies
  • Hamburg has the highest per capita surgery rate in Germany; by serving parts of Schleswig-Holstein and Niedersachsen it inflates its per capita figures and hosts well-resourced hospitals and specialized units.
  • Bremen, despite its smaller population, has a relatively large hospital infrastructure and the highest per capita hospital-bed ratio in Germany; its strong infrastructure attracts patients from surrounding Niedersachsen, supporting high per capita surgery volumes.
  • ⁠Healthcare and medical-equipment manufacturers cannot ignore these key areas. Establishing a presence in these states and metropolitan centres is essential.
  • For manufacturers, distributors, and healthcare service providers, prioritizing states like Nordrhein-Westfalen, Bayern, Baden-Württemberg, and city-states will yield the highest ROI due to patient volumes, infrastructure, and readiness for innovation.
  • Key urban areas in Nordrhein-Westfalen, Bayern, and Baden-Württemberg not only have large populations but also significant commuter inflows. This creates a dynamic patient base with higher elective procedure demand and increased outpatient and day-surgery volumes.
  • Berlin, Hamburg, and Bremen are hubs for specialized care, medical research, and clinical trials. Their high per capita surgery rates make them critical markets for launching innovative devices, pharmaceuticals, and high-tech medical consumables.
  • While urbanized states dominate surgical volumes, other regions may have lower competition but rising demand. Companies can use the concentration in key states as a launchpad while exploring underserved areas for expansion.
  • ⁠High surgical volume and urban concentration of hospitals mean that efficient supply chains, regional distribution centres, and timely service networks are essential. This is particularly relevant for consumables, implants, and high-turnover equipment.
